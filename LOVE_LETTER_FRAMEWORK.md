# Love Letter Framework

## The Thesis

Jenny's north star across every page, every project, every letter:

> *The brief is never the whole problem.*

Every piece of copy should prove this or point toward it.

---

## The Homepage

The homepage is not an archive. It is a thesis with proof.

```
H1          One sentence. What she believes about design.
            Not credentials. Not a greeting.

Her story   Why she sees the work this way.
            2–3 sentences. Unpolished. True.

Honest fit  Right for / not right for.
            Attracts the right people. Filters the wrong ones.

3 projects  myPerks. MegPrime. Roadrunner.
            Each one proves the thesis differently.

CTA         One ask. Schedule 30 min.
```

## The Love Letter

Not a sales pitch. Not their world explained back to them.
Her story, connected to their problem in one sentence.

```
Her belief      What she thinks about this type of problem.
                True regardless of their specific business.
                No speculation. No Googled facts.

Her proof       The project that proves the belief.
                Told as her story. What she found. What shifted.
                What she learned.

The bridge      One sentence. Connects her work to their role.
                Specific. Not generic.

The ask         Direct. Short. No performance.
```

## Case Study Framing

| Don't say | Say instead |
|---|---|
| The brief was... | What I found was... |
| We redesigned the... | Here's what changed when... |
| The client wanted... | The stated problem was X. The real problem was Y. |
| Solution: | What I learned: |

Every case study answers one question:
**What did you see that nobody else was looking for?**

---

## What Makes a Love Letter Work

**Specificity.** Not generic. The detail that shows you actually did the work.
A love letter that says "I love how you make me feel" is fine.
A love letter that says "I love how you laugh at your own jokes and then apologize for laughing" hits different.
Specificity is what makes it real.

**Story.** Not just "I love you" but "here's when I knew."
Bad news. Good news. So what.
Here's when I thought things were falling apart.
Here's the moment everything shifted.
Here's why it matters that I'm telling you this now.

**Honest.** No performance. No trying to sound like something you're not.
People can feel when you're performing versus when you're actually present.
The best love letters are unpolished. Just showing up as yourself.

**Doesn't do too much.** Not a sales pitch.
Not trying to convince anyone of anything.
Just: here's what I see, here's why, here's what you're getting.
Simple. Direct. True.

---

## Never

- Explain their business back to them
- Lead with credentials
- Use metrics as the hook (save them for the proof)
- Sound clever. Sound true instead.
- Speculate about their internal reality
- Em dashes

## Rakuten — Built on the Framework

**Her belief**
Most loyalty programs don't fail because the rewards are bad. They fail because the value is invisible to the person earning it. Show people what they already have, and they behave differently. That is the design problem I keep coming back to.

**Her proof**
At Giant Eagle, four million members were enrolled in a program that was technically generous. Most could not tell you what they were earning. We did not change the rewards. We changed what people could see, and when they could see it. Spend went up 384%. Visit frequency went up 212%.

**The bridge**
That is the problem your Growth squad owns.

**The ask**
I want to work on it at Rakuten's scale. Thirty minutes — let's see if it fits.
