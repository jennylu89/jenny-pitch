# What Top Design Portfolios Do
Research — March 28, 2026

## The meta-finding
Hiring managers spend ~60 seconds on initial pass. The portfolio's job is to earn the deeper read. Every element either earns that read or loses it.

---

## 1. Fewer case studies, not more
Top portfolios show 2–4 projects max. Not 7–10.
- Fewer signals curation and confidence
- Each project gets the depth it deserves
- "I could have shown 10 things. Here are the 3 that best represent how I think."

**Implication for yours:** 7 projects is too many for a hiring manager scan. The pitch-app summary view handles this — but the order matters. Lead with the projects that best match the ICP.

---

## 2. Hook in the first sentence
The first line of every case study does one job: make the reader want the second line.

**What works:**
- Open with the contradiction or the problem at its sharpest
- Avoid scene-setting and background
- No "I was hired to..." or "The client needed..."

**What the best portfolios do:**
- Julie Zhuo: leads with the human tension, not the product specs
- Simon Pan: opens with a number or a striking contrast
- Dan Mall: first sentence = the strategic question the project answered

**Applied:** "The design challenge wasn't managing that complexity. It was making it disappear." beats "MegPrime wanted to build a crypto rent product."

---

## 3. Show the insight, not the process
Portfolios that list steps ("I conducted research, I built wireframes, I ran usability tests") read as junior. What separates senior portfolios:

- The unexpected finding that changed the direction
- The moment where the stated brief turned out to be the wrong problem
- The constraint that became the creative solution

**Applied:** "The brief was a single billing page. But before I touched a screen..." → this shows the instinct to check assumptions before executing.

---

## 4. Numbers, inline and specific
Metrics strips at the bottom are table stakes. The best portfolios weave numbers into the story:
- "28 clicks down to 3" in the prose, not just the metric card
- Makes the story scannable even without reading every word
- Signals that you measure outcomes, not just outputs

---

## 5. Captions carry the story
For visual portfolios: if someone reads ONLY the image captions, they should understand what happened and why it mattered.
- Bad caption: "Billing dashboard redesign"
- Good caption: "Before: 4 separate apps, 28 clicks per invoice. After: one view, one decision."

**Applied to Arena Labs captions:** "6:47am · Pre-shift — biometrics surfaced before first case, Box Breathing recommended based on HRV" — this is a good caption. It tells you the time, the context, and the logic.

---

## 6. Conversational first-person voice
No jargon. No passive voice. No "leveraged," "utilized," "spearheaded."

Top portfolios sound like a smart person explaining their work over coffee, not a resume.

**Voice to avoid:** "Utilized user-centered design methodologies to optimize the conversion funnel."
**Voice to use:** "Users were dropping out at step 3. Nobody had asked why."

---

## 7. Senior signals
What separates a strong senior portfolio from a strong mid-level one:

- **Scope expansion**: Shows you identified that the stated problem was smaller than the real one
- **Business framing**: Decisions connected to revenue, retention, or strategic goals — not just usability
- **Constraints made visible**: Shows you understand the tradeoffs, not just the ideal
- **What you'd do differently**: One honest line about a limit or a learning. Not weakness — maturity.

**Most portfolios skip the last one.** It's a differentiator.

---

## 8. What they skip
- Long process sections with every artifact
- Methodology descriptions ("we used affinity mapping to...")
- Deliverables lists
- "Team collaboration" filler
- Before/after that doesn't explain why the after is better
